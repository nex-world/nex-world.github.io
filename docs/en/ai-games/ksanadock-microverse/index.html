Raise an AI Society on Your Own PC — KsanaDock: Microverse In Box

8 AI characters falling in love, building careers, and forming complete social relationships in your computer — and you, the god watching over this world from above.

👉 Steam Page (Microverse In Box) 
👉 Official Website (KsanaDock) 
👉 GitHub Open Source Repository 

What Is This?

In 2023, Stanford University published a landmark paper — Generative Agents: Interactive Simulacra of Human Behavior. Researchers placed 25 AI characters in a small town and discovered that they would spontaneously socialize, organize events, and even plan a Valentine’s Day party. This later became widely known as the “Stanford AI Town.”

But that experiment required a complex Python backend, making it difficult for ordinary people to run on their own machines.

Microverse In Box (Chinese: 盒中小世界) changed that. It packs an entire AI society into a desktop application built with the Godot 4 game engine — no need to deploy a separate Python service, no LangChain required. Just download, open, fill in your API Key, and your AI town starts running.

🏗️

Game Genre: God-simulation Sandbox. You don’t participate as a character; instead, you observe and intervene in the evolution of this AI society from a top-down perspective.

Who Is KsanaDock?

KsanaDock (时空码头) is an AI-native game project initiated by a solo independent developer.

“Ksana” comes from the Buddhist term “kshana” (क्ण, kṣaṇa), meaning an extremely short unit of time — the name “KsanaDock” evokes the idea that stories unfold in every fleeting moment, and this is the dock where those stories converge.

The developer (also known in the community as “MiaoMiao” or “Gu Yifan YGY”) open-sourced the initial Demo on GitHub in June 2025. Within just 3 days, it garnered 700+ Stars, and quickly climbed to 2300+ Stars. On Zhihu, he wrote an answer to “What kind of open-source project can one person create?” — telling the story of how a solo developer built a multi-agent AI social simulation system from scratch using Godot 4 and LLM APIs.

This is a story about “one person building a world.”

These characters are not static profile panels — they work, chat, argue, collaborate, and over time develop real social relationships.

🧠 Four AI-Driven Systems

1. Independent Thought and Dialogue

Every word and action of each character is generated in real-time by a large language model. There are no fixed lines, no scripted branches — whichever model your API Key unlocks (GPT-4, Claude, DeepSeek…), that’s the “intelligence” and “personality” they get.

2. Long-Term Memory System

Characters remember what happened. Yesterday Tom challenged Grace’s proposal in a meeting, so today Grace is subtly colder toward Tom. Last week Alice helped Jack out of a tight spot, and Jack will remember that favor.

Memories are continuously written to JSON files stored locally — even if you close the game and reopen it, this society doesn’t lose its memory.

3. Autonomous Task Management

AI characters arrange their own schedules autonomously: wake up in the morning → eat breakfast → go to work → chat during lunch → afternoon meetings → socialize after work… They are not puppets waiting for player commands; they are “digital lifeforms” with their own daily rhythms.

4. Environmental Awareness and Reaction

Characters can sense what’s happening around them. If two people are arguing loudly nearby, nearby characters will hear it and react — they might watch, mediate, or quietly walk away.

🃏 Card System (Steam Full Version)

The upcoming Steam full version will introduce a Card System, letting you intervene in this world like a god:

  • Modify a character’s personality and traits
  • Adjust wealth and resource distribution
  • Change relationship status between characters
  • Insert events and plot twists
  • Customize the underlying rules of the world

Want to see socially anxious Tom suddenly become an extrovert? Curious what would happen if Alice won the lottery? That’s just one card away.

Technical Highlights: Why It Deserves Attention

Everything in One Package

Unlike the original Stanford AI Town implementation, Microverse In Box implements all logic inside the Godot 4 engine:

  • ✅ No additional Python service required
  • ✅ No LangChain or similar frameworks needed
  • ✅ Download and play out of the box
  • ✅ Supports Windows / macOS / Linux / Android

Supports Nearly All Major AI Models

Players can freely choose their backend model, or even switch at any time:

CategorySupported Platforms
Cloud APIsOpenAI (GPT-4/3.5), Anthropic Claude, Google Gemini, DeepSeek, ByteDance Doubao, Moonshot AI Kimi
Local DeploymentOllama (run open-source models like Llama, Qwen, etc.)

This means: If you value privacy or don’t want to spend money, you can use Ollama to run open-source models locally, completely offline. If you want maximum character “intelligence” and dialogue quality, you can use GPT-4 or Claude for the best experience.

Fully Open Source + MIT License

The project uses the MIT open-source license, free for commercial use. The initial Demo is fully open-sourced on GitHub — anyone can download, study, modify, or even build their own AI game on top of it.

Is It Worth Buying?

👍 It’s for you if:

  • You’re curious about the concept of “AI society simulation”
  • You’ve read the Stanford AI Town paper and want to play something similar
  • You enjoy god games where you observe and guide (rather than directly participate)
  • You want to run comparison experiments with different AI models — how does GPT-4 vs DeepSeek shape social evolution in the same scenario?
  • You have a soft spot for open source and indie development

👎 You might want to think twice if:

  • You’re expecting a polished, story-rich 3A commercial game
  • You don’t want to prepare your own API Key or configure models (the game doesn’t include AI models — you bring your own)
  • The idea of “watching AI play itself” as a spectator doesn’t appeal to you

An Observation: Why Is It Addicting?

If you’ve seen The Truman Show or Westworld, you’ll understand the unique charm of this experience — watching those pixel characters busily go about their day, and you can’t help but start imagining their stories.

“Why is Tom sitting alone in the corner today? Did he have a fight with Joe yesterday?"
"Alice and Jack have been interacting more lately… is something brewing?”

This projection psychology is an innate human instinct. When you know that every character on screen has an independent AI brain, real memories, and autonomous behavioral logic, the immersion of “they’re truly living in there” far surpasses traditional scripted NPCs.

Closing Thoughts

If History Simulator: Chongzhen shows you how AI can drive the grand narrative of a dynasty’s rise and fall, then Microverse In Box is the opposite extreme — it lets you witness, at a micro scale, the everyday flow of an AI society.

One concerns the fate of a nation, the other the dynamics of the human heart. Together, they form the complete picture of AI-native games across the dimensions of “grandeur” and “everyday life.”
