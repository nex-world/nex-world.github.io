AI Games Are More Than One Thing: From Talking With AI to AI Building Worlds for AI

Ask people what an “AI game” looks like and you will get very different answers.

One person imagines a character they can talk to freely. Another thinks of a small game generated from a single prompt. Someone else pictures an AI teammate that can coordinate, improvise, or deceive. Researchers may instead think of agents training in Go, StarCraft, or a simulated world.

All of these are called AI games, yet they do not fit neatly into one genre.

The problem is not that the term is too broad. It is that we usually ask only one question: does the game contain AI?

Three questions produce a much more useful map:

  1. Who creates the game? A person, an AI, or both together?
  2. Who plays the game? A person, an AI, or a mixed group?
  3. What does AI do inside the game? Generate content, play a character, enforce rules, act as a teammate, or pursue victory for itself?

Once these dimensions overlap, AI gaming stops looking like a single new category. It becomes an entire emerging ecosystem.

1. Talking With AI Can Be a Game

The lightest form of AI game may need no map, skill tree, or combat screen. A conversation between one person and one AI character can already produce a complete game experience.

A player might need to persuade a stubborn guard to let them pass, find contradictions in a suspect’s answers, or slowly earn a character’s trust. The interface is still a chat box, but the player is reading the situation, choosing a strategy, and accepting consequences.

Still, conversation is not automatically a game. Open-ended dialogue usually needs several other elements before it becomes one:

  • A legible goal, such as persuasion, investigation, escape, or relationship building
  • Feedback on each action, such as a shift in attitude, a new clue, or a worsening situation
  • Constraints involving time, resources, identity, rules, or a character’s beliefs
  • Outcomes that are uncertain without feeling arbitrary
  • A continuous process in which the player can learn and adapt

This is why character chat, an AI tabletop campaign, and an AI negotiation simulator can look similar while feeling completely different. The important distinction is not merely whether the model sounds human. It is whether a perceptible, inferable, and responsive game state exists behind the dialogue.

When natural language becomes the control scheme, the narrative medium, and the strategy space at the same time, the chat box is no longer just an input field. It is a new kind of controller.

2. People Playing Games Made by AI

The second form begins before play: AI enters the development process.

It can generate code, art, music, levels, and dialogue, or turn a short description into a working prototype. The eventual player is still human, and the finished product may be indistinguishable from a conventional game. In this case, AI is primarily a production tool.

But “made by AI” covers two different processes:

FormWhen AI worksWhat the player receives
Pre-producedAI generates assets, code, or levels during development; people select and publish themA relatively fixed product
Generated liveAI keeps generating in response to the player, context, and history during playA game that can change every session

The first lowers the barrier to production and lets more people express ideas. The second begins to alter the nature of the game itself. Players are no longer consuming only what a developer placed in a data set; their play causes new material to be created.

This distinction matters because more content does not necessarily mean more gameplay. If AI endlessly generates quests with different names but the same structure, it adds scale, not diversity. Valuable generation changes rules, relationships, strategy, or narrative in ways that matter to the player.

3. People Playing Games Rich in AI Mechanics

AI does not have to author the game. It can instead operate as a set of mechanics inside it.

It may be an NPC that remembers the player, a director that manages the dramatic pace, a referee that understands natural-language actions, a dungeon master that invents tasks, or an enemy that learns the player’s habits. AI does not make the whole game in these examples, but it changes how the game responds.

These uses form a spectrum from shallow to deep:

  • Decorative layer: generated lines, portraits, or backstories that leave the core game unchanged
  • Interaction layer: NPCs converse freely and teammates understand natural-language commands
  • System layer: AI decisions change resources, relationships, quests, and world state
  • Core layer: remove the AI and the main form of play no longer works

Our AI-Native Games Panorama Report explores this technical depth in more detail. From a player’s perspective, however, the real question is not how often a game calls a model. It is whether AI creates a decision space that could not previously exist.

An NPC that talks endlessly but cannot affect the world may still be elaborate scenery. An AI teammate that says little but understands a tactic and shares its consequences may represent a genuinely new mechanic.

4. People and AI Playing Together

The relationship changes when AI moves from the system to the player seat. It no longer only generates the world; it becomes an actor within that world.

Human-AI play commonly takes at least four forms:

  • Teammates: the person handles goals and judgment while AI supplies execution, observation, or complementary skills
  • Opponents: AI learns, deceives, negotiates, and adapts its strategy to the player
  • Host and player: AI manages rules and the world while a person drives the story; sometimes they exchange roles
  • Co-creators: the person contributes intent, AI expands the possibilities, and each takes turns changing the world

Computer-controlled characters have always acted in games, but generative AI gives “playing together” a different meaning. A person can invent a code word, revise a plan, or explain a reason on the spot. AI can propose an approach that never appeared in a walkthrough. Shared play moves from executing preset commands toward negotiating intent and coordinating action.

The ideal AI companion should not simply be stronger. A teammate that never misses and knows every answer takes participation away from the player. A good teammate understands when to lead, when to step back, and when to make a plausible mistake. In human-AI play, legible cooperation often matters more than maximum intelligence.

5. AI Playing Traditional Games

Another path puts AI in the seat originally designed for a human: playing Go, poker, real-time strategy games, open worlds, and other video games.

These games were not built for AI. Their rules, interfaces, and victory conditions were made for people, so AI must observe, plan, and act under the same constraints. Traditional games therefore become test beds for intelligence:

  • Can an agent understand the current situation from images and text?
  • Can it make a plan that spans dozens of steps?
  • Can it manage risk when information is incomplete?
  • Can it communicate with teammates and adjust after failure?

Watching AI play a familiar game also becomes a new kind of spectator experience. We are not watching only for the result. We are watching a different kind of actor interpret rules we already understand. It may uncover a new strategy or fail spectacularly at common sense. That difference has drama of its own.

6. AI Playing Games Designed Specifically for AI

If the player is an AI, a game may no longer need buttons, tutorials, polished graphics, or even a pace that humans can follow in real time.

Games designed for AI can focus on long-term planning, tool use, resource exchange, multi-agent cooperation, social trust, or open-ended exploration. Their “interface” may be an API. Time may be compressed or accelerated. Winning may no longer reduce to a single score.

Such games usually serve three purposes:

  1. Training grounds: AI can practice action and cooperation in a low-risk environment.
  2. Evaluation grounds: stable rules make it possible to compare the capabilities and behavior of different agents.
  3. Social laboratories: researchers can observe multiple AIs forming divisions of labor, alliances, rivalries, and perhaps cultures.

Humans may not be players in these worlds, but they can still be designers, referees, or spectators. The game develops two layers of experience: the rule-governed world experienced by the AIs, and the narrative people perceive while observing their behavior.

This raises a useful question. If only AI can participate effectively while people can merely read logs or watch replays, is it still a game? The answer depends on whether we understand a game as a product, an experience, or a system that produces behavior through rules.

7. When AI Starts Making Games Too

Crossing the creator with the player gives us a fuller map:

Who createsFor peopleFor AIFor people and AI together
PeopleTraditional games and human-designed AI-native gamesAgent training environments and evaluation gamesCooperative games with AI teammates, opponents, or hosts
AILevels, stories, and complete games generated for a player’s requestAI-generated tasks that other AIs attempt to solveCooperative challenges adapted to the abilities of both sides
People and AIPeople set the theme and taste; AI expands the content; people curate and revisePeople define values and boundaries; AI generates and iterates test environmentsBoth sides co-create the rules, world, and next round of play

The most familiar cell is “AI makes games for people.” A player describes a desired world, difficulty, and duration, and AI generates it on the spot. Future game libraries may include not only fixed works but temporary games created for one person, one evening, or one party.

“AI makes games for AI” looks more like automated problem setting and solving. One AI designs a maze, mission, or economy; another tries to master it; the designer adjusts the difficulty in response. Without an external objective, this loop can easily become self-referential. People still need to decide which capabilities are worth developing, which behavior is unacceptable, and what counts as real novelty.

“AI makes games for people and AI together” may be the most promising. A game can understand both the human’s preferences and the AI teammate’s abilities, then create a task that only their cooperation can solve. Rather than inserting AI into an existing team, it can design around their differences from the outset: people are good at value judgments, association, and changing their minds; AI is good at retrieval, parallel processing, and consistent execution. The rules themselves can make both sides necessary.

8. Real Diversity Means More Than Generating More Content

The diversity of AI games appears on at least five levels:

  • Diversity of roles: AI can be author, actor, referee, director, teammate, opponent, or spectator
  • Diversity of rules: rules can be fixed before play or negotiated and evolved during it
  • Diversity of time: one session can last five minutes, or agents can maintain the same world for months
  • Diversity of participants: a game can serve one person, a group of people, a society of AIs, or a mixed population
  • Diversity of purpose: beyond entertainment, games can train, evaluate, create, research, and build relationships

To decide whether an AI game is genuinely novel, it helps to ask five more precise questions:

  1. Would the experience still work if the AI were removed?
  2. Does AI add more assets, or does it create new decisions?
  3. Can players understand AI feedback and form a strategy around it?
  4. Does uncertainty come from meaningful interaction, or merely from randomness and loss of control?
  5. Are the different strengths of people and AI actually expressed in the rules?

These questions are also a reminder: generation is not automatically creation, and intelligence is not automatically fun. Games still need boundaries, pacing, feedback, and taste. AI can expand the space of possibility, but deciding which possibilities are worth keeping remains the heart of design.

Conclusion: From a Type of Product to a Type of Relationship

We have traditionally classified games by platform and genre: console or mobile, role-playing or strategy, shooter or simulation. AI changes something more fundamental. It rearranges the relationships within a game.

People can talk with AI and turn conversation into a contest. They can enter worlds made or continuously generated by AI. AI can join traditional games or compete under rules created only for agents. AI can also create the next game for people, for other AIs, or for both to play together.

None of these forms will replace the others. Fixed rules can still produce exquisite design. Human authorship still carries deliberate expression. Traditional games do not lose their value because NPCs become more intelligent. What is changing is simpler and more profound: there are now more answers to who can create, who can participate, and who can change the game.

The future of AI games may not belong to one standard form. It belongs to an expanding network of relationships, and we are only beginning to learn how to play within it together.
